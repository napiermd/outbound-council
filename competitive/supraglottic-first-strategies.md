## Positioning
SGA-first strategies prioritize oxygenation, simplicity, speed, and minimizing interruption, especially in EMS and cardiac arrest contexts. They may reduce emphasis on ETI/VL expansion.

## Claims to track
- SGAs provide effective conduits for oxygenation and ventilation.
- Airway approach should fit clinician skill, patient condition, and resources.
- Airway management should not interfere with other resuscitation priorities.
- SGA-first use in non-cardiac arrest EMS patients appears to be an active research topic.

## Likely buyer appeal
- Easier training and deployment.
- Less complex than ETI for many providers.
- Strong fit for cardiac arrest or low-frequency intubation systems.
- Clear protocol simplicity.

## IntuBlade counter-positioning
- Do not fight SGA as a rescue or primary tool.
- Position IntuBlade where ETI remains part of protocol, where visualization changes quality, or where review/training makes advanced airway safer.
- Frame as complementary: airway readiness includes BVM, SGA, ETI, suction, capnography, and escalation.

## Evidence gaps / risks
- EMS airway algorithms differ widely.
- For some agencies, SGA-first may be the right operational choice.
- Avoid implying every agency should increase intubation frequency.

## Outbound-safe language
- "If your protocol is SGA-first, IntuBlade may still matter for the subset of airways where ETI is indicated, escalation is required, or training/QI needs visual review."
