# Direct Laryngoscopy

## Source links
- Emergency VL vs DL meta-analysis: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10935931/
- JAMA OR VL vs DL trial: https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/2816267
- NAEMSP airway training statement: https://naemsp.org/position-statement/prehospital-airway-management-training-and-education/

## Positioning
Direct laryngoscopy is the entrenched default: low cost, familiar, durable, no screen/battery, teaches foundational skill, and is embedded in airway culture.

## Claims to track
- Adequate for trained clinicians.
- Cheap and immediately available.
- Skill-retention importance.
- No dependency on camera, screen, battery, or electronics.

## Likely buyer appeal
- Low procurement cost.
- Existing protocols and training.
- Clinician comfort and tradition.
- Works as fallback when video is obscured or unavailable.

## IntuBlade counter-positioning
- Do not position as "DL is bad." Position as "DL skill remains important, but video access should not be scarce."
- Emphasize training, team visualization, reviewability, and coverage for variable operators and high-risk attempts.
- Keep DL as backup/foundation in clinical training language.

## Evidence gaps / risks
- VL evidence varies by setting, operator, blade type, and patient context.
- Avoid universal first-pass superiority claims.
- Respect skill retention and fallback requirements.

## Outbound-safe language
- "This is not an argument to abandon direct laryngoscopy. It is an argument that teams should not reserve video visualization for only certain rooms or rare rescue scenarios."
