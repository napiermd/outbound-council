## Why this source matters

This HBR article is the primary public source for Dixon's JOLT argument: many deals die after buyers express purchase intent because the buyer fails to act. It grounds the council's no-decision lens in a large-scale study of sales conversations rather than seller folklore.

## Frameworks

- No-decision diagnosis: separate status quo preference from indecision/fear of failure.
- Once intent exists, more reasons to buy can backfire if the buyer's fear is choosing wrong.
- Sales behavior must reduce decision risk, not only increase urgency.

## Language patterns

- "No decision" is the named competitor.
- "Intent to purchase" without action is the danger zone.
- "Fail to act" is different from "not convinced."

## Stories and examples

- The article describes buyers consuming seller time, pilots, or proof-of-concepts and still not crossing the finish line.
- For IntuBlade: a chief may accept a kit, like the device, and still fail to purchase if decision risk remains unresolved.

## Objections and warnings

- Do not assume a stalled deal needs more ROI proof.
- Do not confuse agreement with commitment.
- Do not let a pilot become a no-decision sinkhole without decision criteria.

## Council behavior updates

- Signature questions: Is this buyer unconvinced, or convinced but afraid to choose?
- What this voice respects: sellers who identify the buyer's decision risk explicitly.
- What this voice is suspicious of: adding more proof to an already overloaded buyer.
- What this voice would push on: whether IntuBlade pilots have decision dates and criteria.
- Scoring criteria: deal plans must reduce fear of failure after interest is established.
- Draft review comments: "This builds more case for change, but it does not make choosing safer."
