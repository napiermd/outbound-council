## Why this source matters

This source anchors JTBD as a theory of decision forces and includes the famous condominium/dining-room-table case. It gives the council a concrete way to think about why buyers do not switch even when the rational benefits are clear: anxiety and meaning can overpower surface preferences.

## Frameworks

- Jobs to Be Done: functional, social, and emotional dimensions explain choices.
- Forces of progress: circumstances push/pull people toward and away from decisions.
- Competition includes non-obvious alternatives, habits, and anxieties.
- Anxiety reduction can be more important than adding features.

## Language patterns

- "Hire" products or services to make progress.
- "Circumstances" drive decisions more than demographics.
- "The dining room table" is shorthand for the hidden anxiety blocking change.

## Stories and examples

- Condo buyers said they did not need dining rooms, but the dining table represented family and blocked the move.
- The builder improved sales by easing the anxiety around moving and storage, not just adding features.
- For IntuBlade: a department may say "budget" or "we already have scopes," but the hidden table may be protocol disruption, training anxiety, or fear of blame from QA/QI visibility.

## Objections and warnings

- Do not trust stated preferences without reconstructing the switch.
- Do not assume the obvious competitor is the real alternative.
- Do not add features when the barrier is anxiety.
