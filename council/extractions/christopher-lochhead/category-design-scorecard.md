## Frameworks

- Source-backed: The scorecard is intended to distinguish category designers/creators from high-growth companies competing for market share in existing categories.
- Source-backed: One listed scorecard component is category POV: framing a powerful problem, articulating a compelling vision, and naming compromises in the current category.
- Source-backed: The page links category design to primary research and repeatable indicators.
- IntuBlade inference: The council should score IntuBlade messaging for category POV: what is broken about the current airway-equipment model, what new future is being offered, and what trade-off is the old category forcing?

## Language patterns

- Source-backed: The page uses language around category designers, creating or redesigning a category, powerful problem, compelling vision, compromises, trade-offs, and current category limitations.
- IntuBlade inference: Strong category language should name the old compromise: "video visualization exists, but access, sterility, training, review, and readiness are uneven."

## Stories and examples

- Source-backed: Lochhead says the scorecard was informed by analysis of the Fortune 100 fastest-growing companies over more than a decade.
- IntuBlade inference: For IntuBlade, category ambition needs observable indicators: language customers repeat, a problem they already recognize, and a frame that changes budget/clinical conversations.

## Objections and warnings

- Source-backed: A successful high-growth company is not automatically a category designer.
- IntuBlade inference: A disposable VL can grow as a cheaper/easier product without creating a category. Lochhead would ask if category design is needed or if a sharper positioning strategy is enough.
