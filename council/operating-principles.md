# Operating Principles

## Do not flatten disagreement

The council is valuable because April Dunford, John McMahon, Bob Moesta, Bob Wachter, and Omar Khateeb would not always look at the same problem the same way.

Show the useful disagreement. Then make a recommendation.

## Source-backed, not celebrity-backed

A profile is only useful if it is grounded in actual source material: books, talks, interviews, essays, podcasts, or social output.

## IntuBlade is not one business model

The council must handle several overlapping motions:

- EMS disposable video laryngoscope adoption
- hospital and value-analysis entry
- physician-founder narrative
- software and QA/QI dashboard expansion
- computer vision and procedural guidance
- clinical AI and high-trust deployment

## Buyer realism beats clever copy

For EMS, hospital, and clinical buyers, cleverness is fragile. Trust, specificity, workflow fit, proof, and risk removal matter more.

## The deployment kit is a motion, not a giveaway

Treat the kit as the first product experience and the first evidence-gathering step. Outbound should get the right department to evaluate the right kit with the right internal stakeholders.

## The council should produce next actions

Every answer should end with something usable:

- a message
- a deal inspection
- a list-building rule
- a source to verify
- a revised offer
- a buyer-specific next step
