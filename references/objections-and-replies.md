# Objection Handling and Reply Playbook

## When someone replies

A reply is a win. Even "not interested" means they opened the email and cared
enough to respond. Handle every reply with respect.

**Response time:** Reply within 4 hours during business hours. Same day at minimum.
Speed signals you're a real person, not a drip campaign.

**Tone:** Match their energy. If they're brief, be brief. If they ask detailed
questions, give detailed answers. Never be longer than necessary.

## Common objections and how to handle them

### "We already have [competitor product]"

They probably have limited coverage. Ask about it.

**Response angle (Rackham SPIN):** Ask about coverage, not competition.

> What are you using? If you don't mind me asking, is [their product]
> available on every [unit/team/location] or shared? I built [product]
> because most [buyers] I talked to had great [competitor] at [central location]
> but nothing going out on [every deployment].

**Do NOT:** Trash their current solution. Respect their decision. Ask about
their setup, don't tell them it's wrong.

### "Not interested" / "No thanks"

Short and respectful. Don't argue.

> Got it. Appreciate you getting back to me. If [product category] comes up
> down the road, I'm here.

**That's it.** One sentence. Move on. Mark as "Not Interested" and don't
email again unless they reach out first.

### "Send more information"

They're interested but not ready to commit. Send your best asset and move
toward a trial/demo.

> Here's [your best demo/video/one-pager link].
>
> Happy to send a [trial/demo/sample] so your team can try it hands-on.
> What's the best shipping address?

**Goal:** Convert "send info" into a trial. The product sells itself.

### "How much does it cost?"

Be direct. Don't dodge the price question.

> $[price] per [unit]. A [trial/demo] is on us, no cost. Your team tries
> it, you decide.

If they push back on price, reference the competitor comparison from
your company/COMPANY.md file.

### "I need to run this by [someone]"

Find out who and offer to loop them in.

> Makes sense. Who else would be involved in a decision like this?
> Happy to send them info directly or jump on a quick call if that's easier.

### "We don't do enough [volume] to justify this"

Low volume is actually your angle if the product helps with skill maintenance
or coverage gaps.

> That's exactly why [product] matters. When your team only [does X] a few
> times a year, having the right [tool/equipment] for each one matters more,
> not less.

### "Budget is tight" / "No budget"

If you have a free trial or no-cost offer, lead with that.

> That's why the [trial/demo] is free. No PO, no budget approval needed.
> Your team tries it. If it works, we talk about volume pricing when budget
> opens up.

### "We're locked into a contract with [competitor]"

Respect it. Ask about timeline.

> Understood. When does that contract come up for renewal? I'd love to get
> [product] in front of your team before then so you have a comparison point.

### "Can I see a demo?"

If your trial/sample IS the demo, redirect. Otherwise, book it.

> That's exactly what the [trial kit] is. I'll send your team [contents]
> and they try it in their real workflow. Better than a 10-minute demo.
> What's the best shipping address?

### "Can you call me instead?" / "Let's set up a call"

This is a buying signal. Book it immediately.

> Absolutely. Here's my calendar: [calendar link]
>
> If none of those work, just tell me a day and time.

Escalate to the founder/CEO for high-value prospects.

### "Who are you?" / "How did you get my email?"

Be transparent. You found them through legitimate research.

> I'm [credential/role]. I found your [organization] through [honest source:
> website, industry directory, public records]. I [built/represent] [product]
> because [genuine reason].

## Follow-up sequence

### After the cold email (no reply)

**Follow-up #1:** 5 business days after the first email.
Keep it short. Reference the original. Add ONE new piece of value.

**Follow-up #2:** 10 business days after follow-up #1.
Final touch. Shorter than #1. Clear this is the last email.

**After follow-up #2:** Stop. Do not send more emails. Mark for
re-engagement in 6 months.

## Competitive landscape

Document your competitors in company/COMPANY.md. For each:
- Their product and price point
- Their strengths (be honest)
- Your differentiation (not "we're better" — what's categorically different?)
- The positioning angle (different category, not a replacement)

**General principle (Klaff):** Don't position against competitors.
Position as a different category. Never trash the competition.

---

## Disqualification criteria

Document in company/COMPANY.md which prospects should NOT be contacted:
- Already a customer
- Wrong industry/segment
- Explicitly requested no contact
- No verifiable email address
- Too small / wrong fit for your product

When in doubt, ask your manager.

## Reply classification (for automated tracking)

| Type | Detection | Action |
|------|-----------|--------|
| Out of office | "out of office", "auto-reply", "on vacation", "will return" | Log, no status change, retry after return date |
| Wrong person | "wrong person", "no longer with", "retired", "doesn't work here" | Mark email invalid, find correct contact |
| Unsubscribe | "stop", "remove me", "not interested", "no thanks" | Do-not-contact, never email again |
| Wants info | "send more info", "tell me more", "interested" | Warm lead, send assets + push toward trial |
| Wants call | "call me", "let's talk", "schedule" | Hot lead, book immediately |
| Real reply | Substantive response with questions | Warm lead, draft personalized response |
