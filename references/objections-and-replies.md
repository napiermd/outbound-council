# Objection Handling and Reply Playbook

## When someone replies

A reply is a win. Even "not interested" means they opened the email and cared
enough to respond. Handle every reply with respect.

**Response time:** Reply within 4 hours during business hours. Same day at minimum.
Speed signals you're a real person, not a drip campaign.

**Tone:** Match their energy. If they're brief, be brief. If they ask detailed
questions, give detailed answers. Never be longer than necessary.

## Common objections and how to handle them

### "We already have video laryngoscopes"

They probably have 1-2 reusable scopes shared across the department. That means
most rigs don't have VL on every call.

**Response angle (Rackham SPIN):** Ask about coverage, not competition.

> What are you using? If you don't mind me asking, is it on every ALS rig or
> shared across shifts? I built IntuBlade because most departments I talked to
> had great scopes at the station but nothing going out on the truck every run.

**Do NOT:** Trash their current equipment. Respect their decision. Ask about
their setup, don't tell them it's wrong.

### "Not interested" / "No thanks"

Short and respectful. Don't argue. Don't send a follow-up pitch.

> Got it. Appreciate you getting back to me. If airway equipment comes up
> down the road, I'm here.

**That's it.** One sentence. Move on. Mark the lead as "Not Interested" and
don't email again unless they reach out first.

### "Send more information"

They're interested but not ready to commit. Send the YouTube playlist and
the one-pager. Don't overwhelm them.

> Here's a 2-minute video of IntuBlade in the field:
> https://youtube.com/playlist?list=PLD0ZCbkFc8v6c665wroDcq0EtADVnjhgK
>
> Happy to send a deployment kit so your crews can try it hands-on.
> What's the best shipping address?

**Goal:** Convert "send info" into a deployment kit. The kit sells itself.

### "How much does it cost?"

Be direct. Don't dodge the price question.

> $[price per their tier] per blade, single-use. A deployment kit with
> [kit contents] is on us, no cost. Your crews try it, you decide.

If they push back on price:

> A reusable scope runs $4,000 and needs reprocessing after every use.
> At $[price] per blade, you put video on every airway for less than the
> cost of one reusable in a year.

### "I need to run this by [someone]"

Find out who and offer to loop them in.

> Makes sense. Who else would be involved in an equipment decision like this?
> Happy to send them info directly or jump on a quick call if that's easier.

If they mention the medical director and you have the MD's email:

> I actually found Dr. [name]'s contact through [hospital system]. Want me
> to copy them on the next email?

### "We don't do many intubations"

This is actually a strong angle. Low-volume departments have the biggest
skill decay problem.

> That's exactly why video matters. When your crews only intubate [X] times
> a year, first-pass success rates drop without video confirmation. The
> DEVICE trial showed 85.1% first-pass with VL vs 70.8% with direct
> laryngoscopy. That gap is bigger in low-volume agencies.

### "We're locked into a contract with [competitor]"

Respect it. Ask about timeline.

> Understood. When does that contract come up for renewal? I'd love to get
> IntuBlade in front of your crews before then so you have a comparison point.

Don't badmouth the competitor. Don't imply they made a bad choice.

### "Budget is tight" / "No budget for new equipment"

The deployment kit is free. Lead with that.

> That's why the kit is free. No PO, no budget approval needed. I'll send
> [kit contents] and your crews try it. If it works, we talk about volume
> pricing when budget opens up. If not, no hard feelings.

Also check for grant opportunities:
> Have you looked at AFG or SAFER grants this cycle? Airway equipment
> qualifies under operations and safety.

### "Who are you?" / "How did you get my email?"

Be transparent. You're a physician who built a device, not a cold caller
from a lead list.

> I'm an EM physician and former Army combat medic. I built IntuBlade
> because $4,000 reusable scopes don't belong in every fire truck. I found
> your department through [honest source: city website, state EMS data,
> hospital system page].

## Follow-up sequence

### After the cold email (no reply)

**Follow-up #1:** 5 business days after the first email.
Keep it short. Reference the original email. Add ONE new piece of value.

> [Chief title] [last name], I sent a note last week about a no-cost
> deployment kit for [department]. Wanted to make sure it didn't get buried.
>
> [One new detail: a signal you found since, or a nearby department that's
> using IntuBlade, or a relevant study.]
>
> https://cal.com/andrewnapier/intublade-vl-deployment
>
> [signature]

**Follow-up #2:** 10 business days after follow-up #1.
Final touch. Shorter than #1. Clear this is the last email.

> [Chief title] [last name], last note from me on this. If video
> laryngoscopy for [department] comes up in the future, I'm here.
>
> https://cal.com/andrewnapier/intublade-vl-deployment
>
> [signature]

**After follow-up #2:** Stop. Do not send more emails. If they haven't
replied after 3 touches, they're not interested right now. Mark the lead
for re-engagement in 6 months.

### After a positive reply

Move to conversation mode. No templates. Respond to what they said.
Goal: get the deployment kit shipped or a call booked.

### After they receive the deployment kit

**Check-in #1:** 7 days after delivery confirmation.

> [Chief title] [last name], just checking in. Did the deployment kit
> arrive? Happy to answer any questions once your crews have had a
> chance to try it.

**Check-in #2:** 21 days after delivery.

> [Chief title] [last name], it's been a few weeks since the kit
> shipped. How's the team liking it? Any feedback from the crews?

## Competitive landscape

### King Vision (Ambu)

- Reusable video laryngoscope, ~$3,500-4,000
- Requires channeled blades ($30-50 each, also disposable)
- Needs cleaning/reprocessing between uses
- Our angle: similar disposable blade cost, but IntuBlade is the entire
  device at $60-95. No $4,000 handle to maintain.

### McGrath MAC (Medtronic)

- Reusable, ~$3,000-3,500
- Popular in hospital settings, less common in prehospital
- Disposable blades ~$30-40
- Our angle: McGrath is great in the ER. But at $3,000+ per unit, most
  departments can only afford 1-2. IntuBlade puts VL on every rig.

### GlideScope (Verathon)

- Hospital-grade, $8,000-15,000+
- Not really a prehospital competitor but chiefs sometimes reference it
- Our angle: GlideScope is a hospital tool. Different use case entirely.
  IntuBlade is built for the field.

### C-MAC (Storz)

- Hospital-grade, $10,000-15,000+
- Same as GlideScope, not a real prehospital competitor
- Our angle: same as above.

**General competitive principle (Klaff):** Don't position IntuBlade against
these devices. Position it as a different category. They're reusable scopes
that sit at the station. IntuBlade is single-use VL that goes on every truck,
every call. It's not a replacement, it's coverage.

## Disqualification criteria

Do NOT email these departments:

| Reason | How to identify | What to do |
|--------|----------------|-----------|
| Already a customer | Check with Andrew or the dashboard | Skip |
| BLS only (no ALS) | Department website says "BLS transport only" | Skip — they don't intubate |
| Private ambulance service | Company name, not a fire/government department | Skip unless specifically assigned |
| Requested unsubscribe | Previous reply said "remove me" / "unsubscribe" | Skip permanently. Never email again. |
| Hospital-based EMS | Run by a hospital, not a fire department | Skip — different buyer, different process |
| Vet clinic / dental office | Obviously not fire/EMS | Skip |
| No verifiable email | Can't confirm the email from an official source | Skip until you can verify. Don't guess. |

When in doubt, ask Andrew.
