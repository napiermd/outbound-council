# The Public Safety Buyer

## Who you're selling to

Fire chiefs, EMS chiefs, and EMS medical directors are not typical B2B buyers. They operate under constraints that change how every sales principle applies.

---

## Buyer psychology

### Risk aversion is extreme

Public safety leaders make decisions where people can die. Equipment decisions carry clinical, legal, and political liability. A fire chief who buys a bad piece of equipment doesn't just lose money. They face union grievances, council hearings, media scrutiny, and lawsuits.

**Implications for outreach:**
- Hormozi's "irresistible offer" must eliminate risk, not just add value. "No cost, no contract. Your crews try it, you decide." is not a marketing line. It is a risk-removal strategy.
- Cialdini's social proof must be from peers they recognize. "300+ departments" is good. A department in their county or mutual aid network is better. A department their medical director also covers is best.
- Guarantees (Hormozi) are less effective because public safety leaders don't trust "money back" promises from vendors. They trust "try before you buy" because it lets them make their own evaluation.

### Budget cycles are real and rigid

Municipal budgets are set annually. Most agencies plan equipment purchases 6-12 months ahead. If you miss the budget cycle, you wait a year.

- Ohio fiscal year: January 1 - December 31
- Federal grants (SAFER, AFG): announced on rolling basis, but applications are cyclical
- Capital equipment budgets: usually locked by Q3 of the prior year

**Implications for outreach:**
- Timing matters more than persuasion. A perfect email in October hits a locked budget. The same email in March hits planning season.
- Grant signals (SAFER, AFG) indicate fresh money. These leads should be prioritized.
- The deployment kit sidesteps the budget cycle entirely: free means no PO, no budget line item, no council approval. The purchase conversation happens after the crews are already using it.

### Committee decisions, not individual ones

The chief can't just buy. The purchase path:
1. Chief identifies the need
2. Medical director approves the clinical decision
3. Training officer evaluates usability
4. Finance/procurement processes the PO
5. City council or board of trustees approves (above a threshold, typically $5K-$25K)

**Implications for outreach:**
- The cold email is step 0: get the chief interested enough to involve others
- The CC to the medical director seeds step 2 simultaneously
- The deployment kit handles steps 3-4 by getting the device into crews' hands before procurement starts
- Volume deals above the threshold require a different sales motion (proposal, demo, council presentation)

### They talk to each other constantly

Fire departments within a county or mutual aid network share:
- Protocols (often standardized by the regional medical director)
- Training (joint exercises, shared academies)
- Equipment experiences (word of mouth at chief meetings, conferences, state associations)
- Medical directors (one physician often covers 3-10 agencies)

**Implications for outreach:**
- Cialdini's unity principle is powerful. These buyers share a tribal identity.
- One successful deployment in a county can cascade to the entire network.
- One bad experience can poison a region. Quality matters more than volume.
- Medical directors are force multipliers: one MD who adopts IntuBlade brings all their agencies.

## The three buyer personas

### Fire/EMS Chief

**Title:** Chief, Fire Chief, EMS Chief, Assistant Chief, Deputy Chief, Battalion Chief (varies)
**Cares about:** Budget, liability, crew satisfaction, training burden, political optics, maintenance costs
**Email pattern:** Usually .gov (city/township) or .org (fire district). Sometimes personal Gmail for smaller departments.
**Decision style:** Conservative. Wants to see it work before committing. Trusts peer recommendations over vendor claims.
**What moves them:**
- A peer department already using it
- Zero risk (free trial, no contract)
- Reduction in liability (QA/QI data, video documentation)
- Cost savings vs. reusable scopes
- Low training burden (USB-C, plug and play)

**Greeting format:** Always "Chief [LastName]" in cold email. Never first names. Never "Dear" or "Hello."

### Medical Director

**Title:** Medical Director, EMS Medical Director, Associate Medical Director
**Cares about:** Clinical evidence, protocol compliance, first-pass success rates, QA/QI data, intubation training, scope of practice
**Email pattern:** Hospital domain (ohiohealth.com, usacs.com, wright.edu, uchealth.com). Never .gov.
**Decision style:** Evidence-based. Wants data, studies, clinical outcomes. Influenced by peer-reviewed literature and conference presentations.
**What moves them:**
- DEVICE trial data (85.1% VL vs 70.8% DL first-pass success)
- Automatic QA/QI logging (eliminates paper tracking)
- Real-time first-pass rate dashboards
- Peer adoption by other medical directors they respect

**The MD is CC'd, not TO'd.** The email is written to the chief. The MD sees it because they're copied. The QA/QI line is written for them: "Every intubation logs to a QA/QI dashboard so your medical director sees first-pass rates without chasing paper."

### Crews (Paramedics/Firefighters)

**Not directly emailed.** Crews interact with IntuBlade through the deployment kit.
**Cares about:** Does it work? Is it easy? Does it fit in my bag? Will it slow me down?
**Decision influence:** Ground-up. If crews love it, the chief hears about it. If crews hate it, the chief hears about it louder.
**What moves them:**
- USB-C into their existing phone (no extra screen to carry)
- Single-use (no cleaning, no reprocessing, no biomed)
- Clear video quality
- Form factor (fits in a jump bag)

## Government procurement patterns

### Email verification for .gov domains

City and township emails follow patterns, but those patterns have exceptions:
- Most use first.last@city.gov (e.g., michael.rice@daytonohio.gov)
- Some use firstinitiallast@city.gov
- Cities use LEGAL first names, not nicknames (Christopher not Chris, Michael not Mike)
- City IT departments lag behind promotions. The directory may show the old chief's email under the new chief's name.
- Some small townships don't have .gov domains. They use .org, .net, or even Gmail.

**Verification rule:** ALWAYS fetch the actual city staff directory page and confirm the email exists in the HTML before using it. Pattern-guessing produces non-existent mailboxes.

### Grant-funded purchases

Federal grants create buying windows:
- **SAFER grants:** Staffing for Adequate Fire and Emergency Response. Funds new firefighters. Signals growth and budget capacity.
- **AFG grants:** Assistance to Firefighters Grants. Directly funds equipment. If an agency got an AFG, they may have budget for VL.
- **ODNR grants:** Ohio Department of Natural Resources. Wildfire-related equipment.

A grant signal in the opener shows you understand their funding landscape. It is both Cialdini (specificity) and Schwartz (meeting them at their awareness level).

## Regional dynamics (Ohio-specific)

Ohio is IntuBlade's primary market. Key regional patterns:

### Medical direction structure
Ohio law (OAC 4765-3-05) requires every EMS agency to have a medical director. These MDs are typically contracted through hospital systems, not employed by the fire department.

| Region | Hospital System | Email Domain | Coverage |
|--------|----------------|-------------|----------|
| Columbus metro | OhioHealth | ohiohealth.com | 32+ agencies |
| Dayton metro | Wright State / USACS | wright.edu or usacs.com | 10+ agencies |
| Cleveland metro | Cleveland Clinic / USACS | usacs.com | 8+ agencies |
| Cincinnati metro | UC Health | uchealth.com | 5+ agencies |
| Canton area | Aultman | aultman.com | 3+ agencies |
| Toledo area | ProMedica | promedica.org | Unknown |

**One MD, many agencies:** A single medical director often covers 3-10 fire departments. Finding and verifying one MD email can unlock outreach to all their agencies. This is the force multiplier.

### USACS pattern
US Acute Care Solutions (USACS) employs many Ohio EMS medical directors. Email pattern: first_initial + last @ usacs.com (e.g., jaugustine@usacs.com). Domain is accept-all, so email verification returns "risky" not "valid." This is expected. Send anyway.

### Mutual aid networks
Ohio departments within a county share mutual aid agreements, joint training, and often the same medical director. A deployment in one department creates word-of-mouth across the network. Target clusters, not individual agencies.
