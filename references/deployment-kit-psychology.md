# Deployment Kit Psychology

## The kit is the sales motion

IntuBlade doesn't close on the first touch. The first touch gets a deployment kit into a station. The kit sells itself. Every principle below explains why this works and how to maximize it.

---

## Hormozi's Value Equation applied

> Value = (Dream Result x Perceived Probability) / (Time x Effort)

### Numerator (maximize these)

**Dream Result:** Every airway in the field has video confirmation. First-pass rates tracked automatically. Medical director sees QA/QI data without chasing paper. No more blind intubation on a moving ambulance.

**Perceived Probability:** The kit removes uncertainty entirely. "Your crews try it, you decide" means the chief evaluates on their own terms, with their own crews, on their own calls. They don't have to trust a vendor demo. They trust their own experience.

### Denominator (minimize these)

**Time to result:** Kit ships. Blade plugs into any phone via USB-C. First use can happen on the next shift. No implementation project. No IT integration. No training curriculum.

**Effort and sacrifice:** Single-use. No cleaning. No reprocessing. No biomed. No maintenance contract. No capital equipment approval (it's free). No budget line item. No council vote. The chief says "yes, send it" and the next step is automatic.

This is why the kit converts. Every traditional barrier to medical device adoption has been removed.

## The kit tiers

### Tier 1-2 (200+ intubations/year): Enterprise kit
- 12 blades, sizes 3 and 4
- Full app access
- $1,140 value

This is a real deployment. 12 blades across multiple rigs means crews actually encounter it on calls. The sample size is large enough for the chief and MD to evaluate first-pass data.

### Tier 3-4 (<200 intubations/year): Starter kit
- 1 training blade + 2 clinical blades, sizes 3 and 4
- $285 value

This is a taste. Enough for a training session and a couple of real calls. The goal is to get video laryngoscopy on their radar, not to outfit the department.

## Why "free" works differently in public safety

In consumer sales, "free" triggers suspicion ("what's the catch?"). In public safety procurement, "free trial" is the standard evaluation method. Departments routinely receive evaluation units from equipment vendors (apparatus, monitors, PPE). A free deployment kit is not unusual. It is expected.

The key difference: most vendors send one demo unit with a sales rep attached. IntuBlade sends a kit that works independently. No sales rep visit required. No demo appointment. No follow-up pressure. The device speaks for itself.

This aligns with Rackham: the kit opens a conversation. The crews use it, the chief sees the results, and the conversation that follows is about their experience, not your pitch.

## The dollar value anchor

Always state the dollar value of the kit: "$1,140 value" or "$285 value."

Why (Hormozi + Cialdini):
1. **Anchoring (Hormozi):** The dollar value sets the mental reference point. When the chief later hears "$95 per blade," they compare it to the $1,140 they received for free. The per-unit cost feels small.
2. **Reciprocity (Cialdini):** A gift with a stated dollar value triggers stronger reciprocity than an unstated gift. "$1,140 worth of blades" is a bigger psychological gift than "some blades to try."
3. **Concreteness:** A number is more persuasive than an adjective. "Valuable deployment kit" is weak. "$1,140 deployment kit" is concrete.

## Kit placement in the email

The kit offer goes in the **top half** of the email. Always.

The v1 and v2 templates buried the offer in paragraph 4-5. By that point, 80%+ of recipients have stopped reading. The v3 template moves the offer to paragraph 2-3, immediately after the problem statement.

**Structure:**
1. Signal opener (their world)
2. Problem (their gap)
3. **Offer (kit + dollar value + "no cost, no contract")**
4. Credential (one sentence)
5. Question (invites reply)

The offer is the turn. Paragraphs 1-2 build the "why." Paragraph 3 delivers the "what." Everything after is supporting.

## Post-kit conversion path

The kit is step 1. The conversion funnel:

```
Kit received → Crews use on shifts → Chief sees results
    → MD reviews QA/QI data → Internal discussion
    → Chief schedules call (cal.com) or replies to email
    → Volume quote based on actual usage data
    → PO issued → Recurring orders
```

The deployment kit converts skeptics into evaluators. The product converts evaluators into buyers. The QA/QI dashboard converts buyers into recurring customers.

Each stage has different objections:
- **Kit stage:** "Why would I try this?" → Handled by the email (problem + free offer)
- **Evaluation stage:** "Does it actually work?" → Handled by the product and crew feedback
- **Purchase stage:** "Can we afford this?" → Handled by price disruption ($95 vs $15K)
- **Recurring stage:** "Should we keep buying?" → Handled by QA/QI data showing improved first-pass rates

## Conference kit variants

At conferences (SOMA, EMS World, NAEMSP, Pinnacle), the kit offer changes:
- **SOMA 2026:** Buy 2 boxes (20 blades, $1,900), get a free smart device (Android or iPhone)
- **Standard booth offer:** varies by conference

Conference offers use scarcity (Cialdini) and urgency (Hormozi): "At SOMA" or "This week only." The offer is conference-specific and time-bound.

The conference email blast goes to the full attendee list, not individual leads. This means:
- No personalized signal opener (you don't know the recipient)
- Lead with the problem (universal: bulky equipment in austere environments for SOF, general airway gaps for EMS)
- Offer must be concrete and immediately compelling (no "learn more," give them the deal)
- CTA must work for people not at the booth (cal.com booking link, YouTube field footage)
