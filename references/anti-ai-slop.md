# Anti-AI Slop Reference

Your job is to write like a real person. Not like a language model. Not like a marketing
template. Not like a press release. Like a human being who has something specific to say
to another human being.

This file exists because LLMs default to recognizable, formulaic output. Every rule below
comes from documented research on detectable AI writing patterns (Wikipedia's "Signs of
AI Writing" page, peer-reviewed studies, and repeated human feedback).

## The test

Read it out loud. If it sounds like something a real person would say in conversation,
it passes. If it sounds like it was generated by a committee or a content mill, rewrite it.

## Banned words

These are statistically overrepresented in LLM output. Using them signals "a computer
wrote this" to anyone paying attention:

delve, tapestry, vibrant, testament, pivotal, crucial, seamless, bustling, meticulous,
landscape (abstract), interplay, enduring, enhance (generic), showcase (verb), foster,
garner, bolster, underscore (verb meaning emphasize), intricate, multifaceted, nestled,
groundbreaking, renowned, profound, invaluable, robust, innovative, transformative,
leverage (verb), synergy, paradigm, holistic, comprehensive (when used as filler),
streamline, optimize (when vague), spearhead, cutting-edge, game-changer

## Banned phrases

- "I hope this email finds you well" / "I hope you're doing well" — empty filler
- "Best" / "Best regards" / "Kind regards" / "Warm regards" — corporate sign-offs that signal template email. Use your name, or a short closer like "Talk soon" or just your signature.
- "I wanted to reach out" / "I'm reaching out" — you already did. Say why.
- "Just following up" / "Just checking in" — say what you need
- "Don't hesitate to reach out" — nobody was hesitating
- "Please do not hesitate to contact me" — same thing, worse
- "At the end of the day" — filler
- "It's important to note" / "It's worth noting" / "It bears mentioning" — if it's important, say it
- "In today's rapidly evolving..." / "In an era of..." — scene-setting preambles
- "Not just X, but also Y" / "Not only X, but Y" — negative parallelism, an AI tell
- "I'm humbled to share" / "excited to announce" / "grateful for the journey" — performative
- "A testament to..." / "stands as a testament" — inflated significance
- "Serves as" / "stands as" / "acts as" when "is" works — use "is"
- "Boasts" / "features" / "offers" when "has" works — use "has"
- "Despite its [positive], [subject] faces challenges..." — AI outline pattern
- "This matters because..." — hedging setup. Just say why it matters.
- "Setting the stage for" / "marking a shift" / "a key turning point" — generic significance
- "I'd love to connect" / "I'd love to chat" — vague, no commitment
- "Let me know if you have any questions" — lazy close, gives them permission to ignore you

## Banned punctuation and formatting

- No em dashes (the long dash). Use periods, commas, or parentheses.
- No bold text in email prose. Bold belongs in structured docs, not cold outreach.
- No "Key takeaway: Description" bold-colon bullet patterns.
- No triple adjective lists ("fast, reliable, and secure"). Break the pattern.
- No trailing "-ing" summary clauses ("highlighting the importance of," "ensuring a better outcome," "reflecting the growing trend"). This is one of the strongest AI tells. End the sentence before the -ing clause. Or restructure entirely.

## Banned structural patterns

- No "Conclusion" or "In summary" sections. End when the content ends.
- No symmetrical three-paragraph arcs. Real writing is uneven.
- No rhetorical questions as openers ("Have you ever wondered...?")
- No "Additionally" / "Furthermore" / "Moreover" to start sentences. If you need a transition, use a real one or just start the next thought.
- No "elegant variation" — don't keep swapping synonyms for the same thing (device, tool, product, solution, platform). Pick a word and use it.

## What to do instead

**Open with their world.** Not yours. Not a greeting. Not context about you or your company. The first sentence should be about something specific to the person you're writing to.

**Use short sentences.** Mix short and medium. Rarely long. A paragraph can be one sentence.

**Use contractions.** "I'm" not "I am." "Don't" not "do not." "That's" not "that is." Write like you talk.

**Be specific.** Drop real numbers, real names, real details. Specificity is the opposite of AI slop. "Your 18 ALS units running 45,000 calls" beats "your busy department."

**End with a question or a clear ask.** Not "let me know if you have any questions." A real question about their situation, or a specific next step.

**Sign off like a human.** Your name. Maybe "Talk soon" or "Looking forward to it" if genuine. Not "Best regards" or "Warm regards."

## The final filter

Before sending anything, check:
1. Would you actually say this out loud to the person? If not, rewrite.
2. Is there an em dash anywhere? Remove it.
3. Is there a trailing "-ing" clause? Cut it or restructure.
4. Does any sentence start with "Additionally," "Furthermore," or "Moreover"? Rewrite.
5. Are there three adjectives or verbs in a row? Break the pattern.
6. Count the banned words. If there are any, replace them with plain English.
7. Is the sign-off "Best" or "Best regards"? Change it.
8. Does the opening line mention you or your company? Move it. Open with them.
