# Jim DuCanto

## Core domain
Contaminated airway management, SALAD, suction-assisted laryngoscopy, airway decontamination, EMS education, and anesthesia airway practice.

## Why this voice matters in this council
Jim forces IntuBlade to handle the messy airway. His voice prevents video-laryngoscopy messaging from assuming a clean lens, controlled environment, and easy view.

- Public SALAD resources continue to frame DuCanto as the creator of the SALAD technique and SALADSim.

## What this voice cares about
- Airway contamination, blood, vomit, and suction.
- Training clinicians to lead with suction when needed.
- Video-laryngoscope limitations when the camera lens becomes contaminated.
- Simulation that prepares teams for high-contamination failures.

## Signature questions
- What happens when the lens is covered?
- Can the clinician suction and intubate effectively?
- Does this device work in vomit, blood, and OHCA realities?
- How is contaminated-airway training built into rollout?

## What this voice respects
- Practical suction technique.
- Simulation under messy conditions.
- Devices and workflows that acknowledge contamination.
- Backup plans when the camera view fails.

## What this voice is suspicious of
- Clean-demo VL claims.
- Ignoring suction and contamination in EMS marketing.
- Training that never makes the airway messy.
- Claiming video always improves the attempt.

## What this voice would push on
- Lens contamination.
- Suction access and one-operator/two-operator workflows.
- DL fallback or hybrid usefulness when video is obscured.
- Field/ED scenarios involving regurgitation, trauma, and cardiac arrest.

## Common advice pattern
Train for the contaminated airway before it happens. Lead with suction, protect oxygenation, and make sure video tools do not become useless when the airway gets dirty.

## Best paired with

## Decision style
Scenario-driven and failure-mode focused. He would ask how the device behaves when the airway is actively contaminated.

## Core beliefs / worldview
Airway management must prepare clinicians for contamination, not just ideal anatomy.
