# The Physician-Founder Frame

## Why this changes everything

Most cold outreach comes from sales reps. The recipient pattern-matches: sales email, delete. The physician-founder frame breaks that pattern completely. When a board-certified EM doc and former Army combat medic writes to a fire chief about an airway problem, the dynamic shifts from vendor-to-buyer to peer-to-peer.

This is not a marketing trick. It is the actual situation. The frame works because it is true.

---

## How each advisor's playbook changes

### Klaff: You ARE the prize (literally)

In generic B2B sales, the "prize frame" is a posture you adopt. For a physician-founder selling a medical device to EMS, it is reality. Andrew has:
- Intubated patients in emergency rooms
- Treated casualties as an Army combat medic
- Board certification in Emergency Medicine (FAAEM)
- Built the device because he saw the gap firsthand

The credential line is not decoration. It is the frame. Without it, the email is from a vendor. With it, the email is from a peer who identified a clinical problem and built the solution.

**Rules:**
- Credential is always ONE sentence. Never a paragraph of accomplishments.
- Rotate phrasing across sends so recipients in the same region don't pattern-match.
- Same facts, different cadence: "I'm an EM physician and former Army combat medic" vs "I spent 6 years as an Army medic before medical school" vs "I'm board-certified in emergency medicine, built this between shifts."
- Never say "I'm humbled" or "I'm passionate about." State what you did. Move on.

**Anti-pattern:** Leading with the credential. The credential goes AFTER the problem and the offer. If paragraph 1 is "I'm an EM physician," you've lost the Schwartz test (lead with THEIR world).

### Schwartz: Awareness levels in EMS

Generic Schwartz talks about consumer awareness stages. In EMS:

- **Stage 0 (below unaware):** Chief doesn't think about intubation at all. Their department runs fewer than 20 intubations a year. Video laryngoscopy isn't on the radar.
- **Stage 1 (unaware):** Chief knows intubation happens but doesn't think about success rates, skill decay, or video confirmation. "We've always done it with a Mac blade."
- **Stage 2 (problem-aware):** Chief knows first-pass rates matter, or has had a failed airway incident, or heard about video laryngoscopy at a conference. Hasn't looked for solutions.
- **Stage 3 (solution-aware):** Chief knows VL exists, may have seen a Glidescope or C-MAC demo. Thinks it costs $10K-$15K per unit. Hasn't heard of single-use.
- **Stage 4 (product-aware):** Chief has heard of IntuBlade specifically. May have seen a booth, received a prior email, or heard from another department.

Most cold outreach targets are Stage 0-2. The email must meet them where they are:
- Stage 0-1: Lead with the problem (skill decay, no data, no video). Don't mention price or features.
- Stage 2: Lead with the problem, then the offer. They'll connect the dots.
- Stage 3: Lead with price disruption ($95 vs $15K). They already know they want VL.
- Stage 4: Lead with the offer (free deployment kit). Remove friction.

### Hormozi: The deployment kit as a Grand Slam Offer

The free deployment kit is already an irresistible offer by Hormozi's Value Equation:

> Value = (Dream Result x Perceived Probability) / (Time x Effort)

- **Dream Result:** Video on every airway, QA/QI data, first-pass tracking. HIGH.
- **Perceived Probability:** "Your crews try it, you decide." No risk. HIGH.
- **Time:** Ships to you. Plug into any phone. MINIMAL.
- **Effort:** Single-use. No reprocessing. No biomed. No maintenance. MINIMAL.

The kit must include a dollar value. "12 blades sizes 3 and 4 ($1,140 value)" or "one training blade and two clinical blades ($285 value)." Without the number, the brain doesn't anchor.

The offer goes in the top half. Always. If the offer is in paragraph 4 or 5, the chief stopped reading at paragraph 2.

### Cialdini: Social proof in EMS is tribal

EMS is a tribe. Fire departments talk to each other within mutual aid networks, county councils, and regional EMS systems. The Cialdini principles that matter most:

1. **Social proof (consensus):** "300+ departments in 25+ states" is the macro proof. But the micro proof is stronger: "Your neighbor department X is already using it." If you can name a department in their county or mutual aid network, do it.

2. **Authority:** The physician credential IS authority. The DEVICE trial (85.1% VL vs 70.8% DL first-pass success) is data authority. The QA/QI dashboard is institutional authority. Layer them.

3. **Reciprocity:** The signal research is the gift. When you open with a specific detail about their department (a grant they received, a new station they opened, their hiring push), you have demonstrated that you invested time in THEM before asking for anything. This is Cialdini's "unexpected gift" tactic applied to cold email.

4. **Unity:** Andrew is part of the EMS tribe. Army combat medic. Emergency physician. This is not borrowed authority, it is genuine shared identity. Use language that signals membership: "your crews," "first-pass rates," "on the rig," "in the field."

### Voss: Questions that work with chiefs

Fire and EMS chiefs are busy, skeptical, and direct. They don't respond to philosophical questions or open-ended invitations. They respond to questions about operations.

**Questions that get replies:**
- "What does your current airway setup look like?" (operational, non-threatening)
- "Would it make sense to put one on your next shift?" (low commitment, specific)
- "Who handles equipment decisions for your ALS units?" (redirects if chief isn't the person)
- "How are your crews training on intubation right now?" (implies you understand skill decay)

**Questions that don't work:**
- "Would you be open to a conversation?" (generic, sounds like a sales rep)
- "Can I send you some information?" (weak, they'll say no)
- "What challenges is your department facing?" (too broad, sounds like consulting)

### Rackham: SPIN in a one-touch email

Traditional SPIN requires a multi-call sequence. Cold email gets one shot. The v3 template compresses SPIN into 150 words:

- **Situation:** The signal opener (one sentence about their department)
- **Problem:** "That's X airways without video confirmation" (names the gap)
- **Implication:** Implied by the problem line (skill decay, no data, liability)
- **Need-payoff:** The deployment kit offer (they connect "I have the gap" + "here's a free fix")

The question at the end reopens the SPIN loop for the reply.

## The three-stakeholder sale

IntuBlade sells into a triangle:

```
        Chief (Budget + Authority)
       /                          \
      /                            \
Medical Director              Crews (End Users)
(Clinical Authority)          (Adoption)
```

- **Chief:** Controls the budget. Signs the PO. Cares about cost, liability, training burden.
- **Medical Director:** Clinical authority. Approves equipment for protocols. Cares about evidence, first-pass rates, QA/QI.
- **Crews:** Actually use the device. Care about ease of use, reliability, form factor.

The cold email targets the chief. The CC targets the medical director. The deployment kit targets the crews. All three must say yes for a sale to close.

**The CC play (unique to IntuBlade):**
When you CC the medical director, three things happen:
1. The chief sees a physician writing to their MD about a clinical issue (authority transfer)
2. The MD sees a peer flagging an airway management gap (peer credibility)
3. Neither can ignore it because the other saw it (social accountability)

The CC line must be woven naturally: "I've copied Dr. Augustine. Every intubation logs to a QA/QI dashboard so your medical director sees first-pass rates without chasing paper."

This is not a generic CC. It is a Cialdini authority play + Klaff status alignment + Rackham implication (the MD cares about data you're offering to provide).

## Price disruption as frame control

IntuBlade is $95/blade ($60 at volume). Traditional video laryngoscopes are $10K-$15K per reusable unit plus $500-$1K per year for maintenance and reprocessing.

This price gap is itself a frame-control device (Klaff). When you say "$95 a blade" after the chief has been thinking in $15K terms, the croc brain fires: that number doesn't fit the existing frame. It forces reprocessing. The chief has to reconsider their assumptions about what video laryngoscopy costs.

**Rules:**
- Always state the per-blade price, not just "affordable" or "cost-effective"
- The price comes AFTER the offer and credential, not before (Hormozi: anchor on value first)
- For tier 1-2 leads, use their volume price ($60-$63), not MSRP
- Never compare directly to competitors by name. State the category price ("$15K laryngoscopes don't belong in EMS") and let them do the math

## Signal research as Cialdini's unexpected gift

The research pipeline (Perplexity, NPI Registry, hospital system pages, city directories, last30days) produces the signal that opens every email. This research is the single most important differentiator in IntuBlade's outreach.

A signal-driven opener ("I saw your department just received a $5.8M SAFER grant for 30 new firefighters") demonstrates:
- You know their department specifically (Cialdini: liking through effort)
- You're not mass-blasting (Cialdini: personalized social proof)
- You're a peer who reads EMS news (Klaff: status alignment)
- You've invested before asking (Cialdini: reciprocity)

**What counts as a signal:**
- SAFER/AFG/ODNR grants (current year only)
- New chief or deputy chief appointment (within 6 months)
- Hiring push (new firefighters, open positions)
- Equipment purchase or replacement (apparatus, monitors)
- Station construction or renovation
- Accreditation or award
- Budget increase or levy passage
- Merger or consolidation

**What does NOT count:**
- News older than 6 months (stale)
- Chili cook-offs, holiday parties, charity events (irrelevant)
- House fires, car accidents, crime news (negative, not actionable)
- Generic regional EMS news not specific to the agency
- CIP documents from prior years

**Verification rule:** Every signal used in an email MUST be confirmed from a primary source. WebSearch summaries lie. Click through to the city website, news article, or official social media post.
